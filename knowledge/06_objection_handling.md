# Objection handling guide

*For AEs · pattern library, not a script*

---

## How to handle any objection

1. **Acknowledge** — show you heard it. "That's a fair concern" beats jumping straight to the rebuttal.
2. **Clarify** — make sure you understand what they actually mean. "When you say 'too expensive,' is that against the budget you had in mind, or against another vendor?"
3. **Reframe or respond** — not always with an answer. Sometimes the right response is to surface the underlying assumption.
4. **Check** — "Does that address it, or is there more to it?"

The biggest single mistake AEs make: hearing objection → launching into rebuttal. Skip step 1 and 2 and the customer feels unheard.

---

## Common objections

### "Too expensive"

**What it usually means:**
- "I haven't seen enough value to justify it"
- "I have a different vendor in for less"
- "My budget is real, and this is over it"
- "I'm anchoring you down before negotiation"

**Don't** start discounting. Don't ask "what would you need to see to make this work?" — that signals desperation.

**Do** ask: "Compared to what?" or "What were you hoping it would land closer to?" Then either re-establish value or unpack the comparison.

If genuinely over budget: explore phasing, longer commitment for lower year-1, fewer modules in year 1.

---

### "We need to think about it"

**What it usually means:**
- "I'm not the decision-maker and I don't want to admit it"
- "There's something I haven't told you about"
- "I'm not bought in but I'm being polite"

**Do** name it: "Of course — out of curiosity, what's the biggest hesitation right now?" Or: "Help me understand what 'thinking about it' looks like — are there other people who need to weigh in?"

The worst thing you can do is accept "we need to think about it" at face value and disappear for 3 weeks.

---

### "Send us a proposal"

**What it usually means (early in the cycle):**
- "We're shopping for prices"
- "I want to look busy to my boss"

**What it usually means (later in the cycle):**
- "We're ready to evaluate seriously"
- "I need something to put in front of the EB"

**Do** push back when it's early. "Happy to send something, but I want to make sure it actually maps to what you need. Can we walk through the must-haves once more before I draft anything?"

When it's appropriate, send a proposal with a mutual action plan. Always.

---

### "We're also looking at [competitor]"

**Do** ask: "What's drawing you to them?" — without defensiveness.

Listen to the answer. It tells you what the customer actually cares about, often more reliably than direct discovery questions.

Then bridge: "That makes sense — they're strong on X. Where we tend to be different is Y. Would it be useful to dig into that comparison?"

Don't trash the competitor. It makes you look small.

---

### "Your security review looks heavy"

**Often means:** IT or security team has been the bottleneck on previous purchases and is gun-shy.

**Do** offer to have our security team brief theirs directly. Engineer-to-engineer conversations move much faster than AE-to-IT. Have the SOC 2 report and DPA ready.

---

### "We already have [legacy system / Sage / SAP on-prem]"

**Usually genuine — and a signal of buying urgency.**

Probe: "How's that working?" Often the answer reveals real pain. Then: "What would have to be true for you to consider replacing it?"

Don't bash the incumbent — they chose it, and bashing it makes them feel stupid. Acknowledge that it worked for where they were, and ask what's different now.

---

### "Can you match HiBob's price?"

This is procurement testing you. Usually not the EB's voice.

**Do** hold the line. "We price based on the value we deliver, not on matching individual vendors. What we *can* do is talk through where we'd be a better long-term investment — including TCO over 3 years. Would that be useful?"

Discount only with trade — longer commitment, more modules, faster signature, case study rights.

---

### "We don't have budget right now"

**What it usually means:**
- "I haven't built a business case yet" (the real one)
- "We're freezing spend"
- "We have budget but I'm using this as leverage"

**Do** ask: "When does budget typically get planned for projects like this?" and "If we had aligned on this 3 months ago, would it be in the budget now?"

If genuinely no budget — pivot to nurture, set a specific check-in for when budget gets discussed.

---

### "Your onboarding is slow"

(usually means our IM team mentioned a realistic 8-week timeline and they wanted 3)

**Do** ask: "What's driving the urgency on the date?" Often it's an internal deadline that has more flex than they're letting on.

Then: "We can absolutely accelerate — what we won't do is rush in a way that creates payroll risk on day 1. Here's what an 8-week vs 5-week timeline trades off."

Implementation discipline is a feature. Customers who push the hardest on speed are the ones who blow up when payroll runs incorrectly month one.

---

### "I need to discuss with the team"

If you don't know who "the team" is, you don't know your deal.

**Do** ask: "Would it make sense for me to be in that conversation? I can present briefly, then step out so you can discuss." If they say no, fine — but ask: "Who specifically will be in it, and what's the question you'll be deciding?"

Then book the follow-up *while you're on the call*. "Sounds great — let's grab a slot for the day after. Tuesday at 11?"

---

## When to qualify out

Some objections aren't objections, they're "this isn't a fit." Recognise:

- No budget, no timeline, no champion → not a deal
- Wrong size (way under or way over ICP) → not our deal
- Wrong industry / geography → not our deal
- Already happy on a competitor with no triggering event → not winnable

Qualifying out is a skill. The AEs who do it well close more, not less.
