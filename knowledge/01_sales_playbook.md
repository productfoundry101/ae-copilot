# Personio Sales Playbook

*Internal use only · Last updated: Q1 2026 · Owner: Sales Enablement*

A reference for AEs across the full deal cycle. Use what's relevant, ignore what isn't. The playbook is opinionated, not prescriptive.

---

## 1. How we sell

Personio sells to mid-market and enterprise companies in Europe (200 – 5,000 employees primarily). We win by being the HR operating system European companies actually want — local-first, compliance-grade, modular, and fast to deploy.

We lose to point solutions when we try to compete on individual features. We win when we sell the platform play: one place for HR, payroll, performance, and recruiting.

We don't try to be everything to everyone. We don't win on price. We don't lead with feature checklists.

---

## 2. The MEDDIC framework (how we qualify)

Use MEDDIC discipline on every deal above €30k. The qualification fields in Salesforce map directly to this.

**Metrics** — what's the quantified pain or upside? "Saves 12 hours/week per HR person," "reduces payroll errors by X%." If you can't put a number on it, you don't have one.

**Economic Buyer** — who can say yes to a six-figure purchase without asking anyone else? Have you met them? Have you presented to them?

**Decision Criteria** — what are they actually choosing on? Often different from what they say in discovery. Probe.

**Decision Process** — what are the steps from here to signature? Procurement involvement? Security review? Board approval?

**Identify Pain** — pain has to be felt at the level of the economic buyer, not just the champion. A champion's pain doesn't unlock budget.

**Champion** — someone who has skin in the game, will sell internally when you're not in the room, and can answer the procurement questions you don't see.

A deal without a champion is not a deal. It's a hope.

## 3. Stages and what good looks like

### Discovery
You're trying to understand: what triggered them to look now, what they're using today, what's painful, who's involved, what they've already ruled in or out.

Good discovery ends with you knowing more about their problem than they articulated when the meeting started. If they did all the talking and you did all the answering, that wasn't discovery — that was a demo.

**Exit criteria:** named pain, named champion, sense of who the EB is, rough budget signal, clear next step booked.

### Qualification
Decide whether to keep investing. Mutual fit, mutual interest, mutual urgency. If any of those is missing, slow down or qualify out.

**Exit criteria:** MEDDIC fields populated to at least 70%, multi-thread started (more than one stakeholder engaged).

### Demo
Not a feature tour. A targeted demo against the 2–3 specific pains discovery surfaced. Tell the story of their workflow, not ours.

Common mistake: demoing everything we built. The bigger the demo deck, the worse the outcome.

**Exit criteria:** champion can articulate what Personio does for them. Technical questions surfaced and answered. Sense of which competitors they're seriously evaluating.

### Proposal
Don't send a proposal until you've validated pricing range with the EB or champion. Surprise pricing kills momentum.

Always send a written proposal with a mutual action plan attached. Verbal-only proposals slip.

**Exit criteria:** proposal acknowledged, MAP signed, procurement engaged (or skip-able).

### Negotiation
Procurement-led. Hold price wherever possible — concessions on terms (payment, commitment length, ramp) generally beat concessions on price. Document every commitment, every time.

**Exit criteria:** redlines closed, security review complete, signature path clear.

### Closed Won / Lost
Run a 15-minute close-loop call with CSM (won) or a 30-minute lost-deal review (lost). Both are worth more than you think.

## 4. The Three Calls

We talk about discovery, demo, and proposal as discrete events. In practice they blend. But always plan for at least three substantive conversations before proposal.

If a deal goes from intro → proposal in two calls, it's almost always going to slip in negotiation. Either it was already qualified-out internally (and they're price-shopping you), or you skipped multithreading and the EB will surface at the worst possible time.

---

## 5. Multi-threading

The most reliable predictor of deal velocity. The single biggest avoidable cause of late-stage stalls.

Get to 3+ stakeholders by end of demo. Get to the EB by end of proposal. If your champion is blocking you from the EB, that's a yellow flag — find out why.

Activities to multi-thread:
- Joint workshop on their current process (forces them to bring 2–3 people)
- Reference call with someone at their seniority level (forces seniority equivalence)
- Mutual action plan review (legitimate reason to need the EB on the call)

---

## 6. Renewals

Treat every renewal like a new sale. Customer success owns the relationship, but the AE leads the commercial conversation.

Start the renewal motion 90 days out for MM, 120 for ENT. Late starts are how we get squeezed on price by procurement.

Two anchors for the renewal:
- **Adoption and impact** — are they getting value? Numbers if you can.
- **Roadmap and forward fit** — what changes in their world in the next 12 months? What in our roadmap maps to that?

Expansions live inside renewals more often than they live as standalone deals. The renewal conversation is the natural moment to surface what's next.

## 7. Common failure modes

**Single-threaded deals.** One champion, no one else engaged. Champion leaves, deal dies.

**Pricing too early.** Anchoring before you've established value. The customer thinks of you as a price, not a partner.

**Optimism in CRM.** Stages that don't match reality. Forecast slips. Trust eroded with leadership.

**Skipping the executive briefing.** Late-stage deals without exec sponsor on either side stall. Always — *always* — get a real conversation between exec sponsors before signature.

**Discounts without trade.** Every discount must come with something — longer commitment, more product, faster signature, case study rights. Discounts without trade train customers to ask for more.

## 8. What the CRM data tells us

Before any call with an existing account, look at:

- Recent activities (last 60 days) — what's happened, who's been engaged
- Open and recent support tickets — especially P1s and unresolved P2s
- Product usage trend — MAU, logins, module adoption
- Open opportunities — what's in play
- Stakeholder map — who's engaged, who's gone quiet

Before any call with a prospect, look at:

- All historical activity — what we've already learned in discovery
- Stage and days in stage — momentum signal
- Open opportunities — what's been proposed
- ICP fit notes

Ten minutes of CRM prep beats a polished pitch deck for a meandering call.

---

## 9. When to escalate

- Deal at risk and you don't know why → CSM + manager
- Sensitive customer escalation (P1, exec-to-exec) → manager + CS leadership
- Pricing concession beyond approved guidance → deal desk
- Legal redlines beyond standard → legal
- Multi-region / multi-entity complexity → solutions engineering

Don't burn time being a hero. Escalate fast.
